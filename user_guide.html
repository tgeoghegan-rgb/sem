Section 1

What is Driver Analysis?

Driver analysis is a statistical technique that identifies which factors most influence an outcome — for example, which aspects of a customer experience most drive overall satisfaction, or which brand perceptions most drive purchase intent.

This tool uses Relative Weights Regression, a method closely related to Structural Equation Modelling (SEM). It is particularly well-suited to survey data scored on Likert scales (1–5 or 1–10), where respondents rate multiple attributes alongside an overall outcome question.

In plain terms: you're asking "of all the things we measured, which ones actually move the needle on the outcome we care about — and by how much?"

What makes this different from a simple correlation?

A simple correlation tells you which attributes are associated with the outcome. Relative weights regression goes further — it accounts for the fact that survey attributes are often correlated with each other, and partitions the explained variance fairly across all drivers. This gives you a more honest picture of each driver's true independent contribution.

The network

The "network" in this tool refers to the set of driver questions you have selected, all pointing toward a single outcome variable. You can experiment with different network configurations — including or excluding drivers — to understand how the model changes.

Section 2

Key Metrics Explained

R²
0–1
How much variation in the outcome is explained by all drivers combined. 0.70+ is strong; 0.40–0.69 is moderate; below 0.40 is weak.
Importance (%)
RW%
Each driver's relative contribution to R², expressed as a percentage. All drivers sum to 100%. Higher = more important.
Performance
Mean
The average score for that driver across all respondents. Reflects how well you are currently performing on that attribute.
Std Beta (β)
±0–1
Standardised regression coefficient. Shows direction and strength of the relationship. Positive = higher score drives higher outcome.
p-value
<0.05
Statistical significance. Below 0.05 means the relationship is unlikely to be due to chance. Above 0.10 means treat with caution.

Significance flags

Each driver row in the table is colour-coded and flagged as follows:

Colour Flag p-value What it means
✓ Significant < 0.05 Strong evidence this driver genuinely influences the outcome
~ Marginal 0.05 – 0.10 Some evidence, but interpret with caution
✗ Not significant > 0.10 Weak or no evidence — consider removing from the model

Sample adequacy

A warning is shown if your sample size is too small for the number of active drivers. The guideline is at least 10 respondents per driver. With 12 drivers you need at least 120 respondents; with 500 respondents you can comfortably run up to 50 drivers.

Section 3

Reading & Interpreting the Outputs

Driver Importance Table

The table ranks all active drivers from most to least important. Read it top to bottom — the higher a driver sits, the more it influences the outcome. Use the significance flag (colour coding) to judge how much confidence to place in each result.

Tip: A driver can have high importance but low performance — this is your highest priority area for improvement. Conversely, high performance on a low-importance driver is not where to invest resources.

Importance vs. Performance Chart

This is the core strategic output. Each bubble represents one driver, positioned by:

Reading the axes

X-axis → Performance — how well you're currently scoring on this driver (mean rating). Further right = performing better.
Y-axis ↑ Importance — how much this driver influences the outcome. Higher up = more influential.
Bubble size Also represents importance — larger bubbles are more important drivers.

The chart naturally divides into four strategic quadrants:

↑ High importance, high performance

Strengths to protect. These are driving the outcome and you're doing well — maintain focus here.

↑ High importance, low performance

Priority gaps. These matter most to the outcome but you're underperforming — top investment priority.

↓ Low importance, high performance

Potential over-investment. Performing well but it's not moving the needle — consider reallocating resource.

↓ Low importance, low performance

Monitor only. Low priority — not important enough to justify significant investment right now.

Driver Importance Ranking Chart

A simpler ranked bar chart showing each driver's relative importance weight. Use this for presentations and stakeholder communication — it's easier to read at a glance than the bubble chart. The longer the bar, the more that driver is driving the outcome.

Section 4

How to Use the Tool

1
Prepare your CSV file

Each row should be one respondent. Columns should include your Likert-scale driver questions (1–5 or 1–10) and at least one outcome question. Text columns (e.g. region, age band) are automatically treated as segment filters, not drivers.

2
Upload your CSV

Click the upload area at the top of the page and select your file. The app will confirm how many rows and columns it has detected. If no file is uploaded, the built-in sample dataset is used.

3
Select your outcome variable

Use the dropdown in the left sidebar to choose which column is your outcome — for example "Overall satisfaction" or "Purchase intent". All other numeric columns will be available as candidate drivers.

4
Select your drivers

In the sidebar, check or uncheck driver questions to include or exclude them from the model. The summary line shows your current active network (e.g. "9 of 12 drivers → Overall satisfaction").

5
Apply segment filters (optional)

Use the segment dropdowns (region, age band, gender etc.) to filter the analysis to a specific subgroup. The model will re-run on only the filtered respondents.

6
Click Run Analysis

The model runs on your current selections and outputs are updated. Results do not change automatically when you adjust settings — always click Run Analysis after making changes.

7
Compare scenarios

Run the analysis, note the results, adjust your driver selection or outcome, and run again. Use this to compare different network configurations or outcome variables side by side.

Section 5

Tips & Tricks

🎯
Start with all drivers, then prune

Run the analysis with all drivers active first. Then remove any drivers flagged red (not significant) one at a time and re-run. The R² may barely change, confirming those drivers add little — and your remaining results become cleaner.

🔄
Compare outcome variables

Run the same driver set against Awareness, then Consideration, then Purchase. The driver rankings will differ — this tells you which drivers are specifically influencing each stage of the funnel.

🔍
Use segment filters for subgroup insights

Run the full sample, then filter by a specific region or age group and re-run. Compare the driver rankings — what matters most to one segment may not matter to another.

📏
Watch your sample size

When you apply segment filters, your effective sample size shrinks. If the sample adequacy warning appears, treat those results as directional only — not statistically robust.

⚠️
High importance + negative beta = a suppressor

If a driver shows high importance but a negative standardised beta, it may be a statistical suppressor — correlated with other drivers in a complex way. Flag this for a deeper methodological review rather than interpreting it at face value.

📊
R² below 0.40? Check your drivers

A low R² usually means one of three things: your driver questions don't fully cover what's driving the outcome; the outcome question is poorly defined; or your sample is too small. Try adding more drivers or reviewing the survey design.

🔒
Data confidentiality

For client or commercially sensitive data, anonymise your CSV before uploading — remove any columns that identify the client, brand, or individual respondents. The model only needs the Likert scores and broad demographic segments.
